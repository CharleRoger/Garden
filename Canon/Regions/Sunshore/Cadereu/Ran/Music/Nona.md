Nona (*early:* /nɑːnɑ/, *Tottag:* /nonɐ/) was the traditional Ran style of music. The word especially referred to sung music, though did not exclude instrumental music, especially when played as accompaniment to song. Nona was typically performed by the [[tot|totyq]] and by wandering performers. The terms for the latter would typically depend on the instrument played, though it was almost always an accompaniment.

Nona music overwhelmingly used a scale consisting of seven pitches, each with a number. These notes were considered to repeat each octave. The pitch of notes was compared using the metaphor of thickness. For example, *catnigum* would have been considered thinner than *icat*. The system of numbering used with the notes was evidently based on their position in the harmonic series, though written descriptions of it have not survived. It would have been known to theorists through wind instruments, which have been found in graves and settlements. 

Older written down music exclusively uses pitches 1-4. Later practice suggests more pitches would have actually been sung than recorded - singers and players would have applied ornaments and improvisation during the performance, while still following along the main contour as written. When pitches 5-7 finally make their written appearance, this is initially with sections where the ornaments have been explicitly marked down. Some melodies have even been found written in both forms, first with only 1-4 and later on with the full set.

Eventually pitches 5-7 start to be seen in all kinds of written music, even in contours containing lots of unwritten ornamentation for the singer to apply. Nonetheless, pitches 1-5 remain the most common. Theorists' descriptions from this period explicitly recognize seven pitches in total, which becomes canonical. It is at this point pitch 7 receives its nickname, as it is the 7th and thus the last.

However, subtler variations are still recognized when it comes tuning. Theorists talk of thick 6 and thin 6, as well as thick 7 and thin 7. The thick variants are probably those based on the harmonic series, as might be played on a wind instrument. The thin variants have seemingly been altered to make ratios of 5:4 and 3:2 respectively with *nannigum*: "The thin Father-Step is to Mother-Step as Day is to Mother," a theorist from Feculky writes. This is probably for the sake of harmonization, as the development of polyphony commences.
